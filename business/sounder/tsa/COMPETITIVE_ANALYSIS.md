## Company Position

The Tamworth Swine Association is a single-breed registry managing pedigree records for the Tamworth -- a red-gold, long-bodied, lean heritage hog recognized as the oldest pedigree pig breed in the world. TSA maintains approximately 113-114 registered breeders spread across 37 states, making it one of the smallest breed registries in the United States by membership.

The Tamworth is a terminal-purpose breed prized for its bacon qualities. The long, deep side produces lean, well-marbled cuts that command premium prices in heritage and specialty pork markets. Tamworths are hardy foragers bred for pasture-based systems -- they thrive outdoors, perform well on rough ground, and convert forage efficiently. These characteristics position the breed squarely in the farm-to-table, pasture-raised, heritage pork movement that is reshaping premium retail.

TSA operates without a herdmark system, without modern digital infrastructure, and without the institutional resources of larger registries. Its role is narrow: maintain the Tamworth herdbook, promote the breed, and connect breeders. With 113 breeders across 37 states, the average state has roughly three Tamworth breeders -- a thin network that makes breed preservation fragile and market coordination nearly impossible without technology.

### Key Registry Dynamics

- **Scale gap:** CPS has 578x more breeders than TSA. Even ABA, the closest single-breed comparison, has 78x TSA's membership. TSA operates at a scale where individual breeder attrition is existential -- losing ten breeders is a 9% membership decline.
- **No cross-registry infrastructure:** A Tamworth breeder selling crossbred pigs (Tamworth x Berkshire, Tamworth x Large Black) has no pedigree trail connecting the Tamworth parent to the crossbred offspring. Heritage crossbreeding is growing, but the data is invisible.
- **Heritage breed fragmentation:** Each heritage breed operates its own tiny registry with its own paper-based systems. There is no shared infrastructure, no shared technology, and no shared market intelligence across the heritage swine sector.

## Heritage Pork Market

The heritage pork market is the fastest-growing premium protein segment in the United States, driven by consumer demand for farm-to-table sourcing, pasture-raised production, and breed-specific flavor profiles.

### Market Drivers

- **Farm-to-table movement:** Direct-to-consumer sales through farmers markets, CSA programs, and farm stores have created a viable retail channel for small heritage producers who cannot compete in commodity markets.
- **Premium retail demand:** Whole Foods, specialty butchers, and premium restaurant suppliers are actively sourcing heritage breed pork. Breed identity has become a marketing asset -- "Tamworth bacon" commands shelf space that "pork" does not.
- **Pasture-raised certification:** Consumer willingness to pay premiums for pasture-raised, antibiotic-free, non-GMO pork is growing. Heritage breeds raised on pasture check every box the premium consumer is looking for.
- **Heritage breeds outperforming commercial in premium channels:** In commodity markets, heritage breeds lose on feed conversion and days to market. In premium channels, heritage breeds win on marbling, flavor, story, and price per pound. The economics invert completely when the end market values quality over efficiency.

### Tamworth's Unique Position

The Tamworth is specifically valued for bacon. The breed's long body, lean build, and well-distributed fat produce bacon that heritage pork advocates consider superior to any commercial breed. "Tamworth bacon" has name recognition in the heritage food community that most heritage breeds lack. This is a marketable brand asset that TSA has never formalized or leveraged.

## Technology Landscape

### Current State: The Entire Heritage Swine Sector Is Paper-Based

| Function | Heritage Registry State | What Should Exist |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Pedigree registration | Paper forms, mailed checks, manual data entry | Digital registration with photo verification and lineage linking |
| Breeder directory | Static web page or PDF list | Searchable, map-based breeder directory with available stock listings |
| Member communication | Email lists, annual newsletter, Facebook groups | CRM with segmented outreach, SMS alerts, community platform |
| Online registration portal | None across most heritage registries | Self-service portal with payment processing and instant confirmation |
| Analytics and reporting | None | Breed population trends, geographic distribution, genetic diversity metrics |
| Sales and marketplace | Facebook, Craigslist, word of mouth | Pedigree-verified marketplace with heritage certification |
| Heritage certification | No formal system | Breed-verified, pasture-raised certification program tied to registry data |

The technology gap in heritage swine registries is not a matter of outdated systems -- it is a matter of no systems at all. CPS and NSR at least have legacy web portals and digital databases, however dated. Heritage breed registries like TSA, Large Black, GOS, Red Wattle, Mulefoot, and Ossabaw operate on paper records, personal spreadsheets, and volunteer labor. There is no CRM. There is no online registration. There is no analytics. The administrative burden falls on a handful of volunteer officers managing a breed's survival from their kitchen tables.

This is not a technology deficit. It is a technology vacuum. And it means that a single platform could digitize the entire heritage swine sector overnight.

### The Heritage Premium Math

A commodity hog sold at market brings $0.50-0.70/lb live weight. A Tamworth raised on pasture and sold direct-to-consumer as heritage pork brings $8-15/lb retail cuts, with bacon commanding $12-18/lb. A single Tamworth hog yielding 120 lbs of retail cuts at heritage pricing generates $960-$1,800 in revenue -- compared to $125-$175 for the same animal sold at commodity price. The breed premium is not marginal. It is transformational for small producers.

The pasture-raised pork market is growing 15-20% annually, and heritage breeds are the fastest-growing sub-segment within it. Consumer education about breed-specific flavor profiles -- driven by food media, chef advocacy, and farm-to-table storytelling -- is converting commodity pork buyers into heritage pork buyers at a rate that heritage producers cannot keep up with. Demand is outpacing supply.

### Structural Reality

TSA is a 113-breeder registry with no technology, no marketing, no certification program, and no pathway to scale. The breed it protects is uniquely valuable -- the Tamworth's bacon qualities and pasture-hardiness are exactly what the premium pork market demands. But the association lacks every tool required to convert that breed value into breeder growth, consumer awareness, and market capture. The brand asset exists. The infrastructure to monetize it does not.
